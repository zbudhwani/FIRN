Why It's So Hard to Invest in Clean Energy as an Everyday Investor

  • Date : 19th January, 2023
  • Time : 3 Min Read

If someone was to ask you the most straightforward way to invest in real estate, the answer is simple: buy a house.  Yes, there is a laundry list of other ways to get exposure to real estate: publicly-traded real estate investment trusts (REITS), private real estate asset managers (e.g., Fundrise, Cadre), fractional ownership startups (e.g., QuantamRE), companies in the real estate adjacent space (e.g., Zillow, Redfin, and Rocket Mortgage), and companies that supply the materials for building houses (e.g., lumber companies). But those avenues are not substitutes for owning a house. You want to invest in real estate? Then buy real estate. Simple as that.


However, if someone was to ask you what was the most straightforward way to invest in clean energy today, the answer is far less straightforward. Can you own a solar farm? A wind farm? A hydropower plant? Unlikely.  Some might say “well, you can install solar panels,” while others might say to buy stock in Tesla or a solar manufacturer. But if you look closely, that answer is quite different from the answer you might give for real estate.


This article explores why it is so difficult for normal people to invest in clean energy infrastructure as investors and explains how Firn can help.


The limits of residential solar


Take residential solar power as an example.  Yes, installing solar panels on one’s roof is as direct an investment one can make into clean energy.  But unlike real estate, where there are single-family homes and multi-family condominiums available at all kinds of price points, the same is not usually true for residential solar.


One reason for the dissimilarity is that many people rent, rather than own, their own homes. Renters, therefore, may be unable to convince their landlord to install solar panels on their property, assuming the building could structurally support it.  People who live in apartment buildings or multi-family dwellings usually face additional difficulties getting solar installed on the building even when they do own a condominium unit.  Those reasons range from structural impediments, like space availability (e.g., rooftops on multi-unit dwellings often use roof space for HVAC units, electrical systems, and other equipment), to legal restrictions (e.g., covenants and restrictions, HOA rules, and local building codes).  And even for homeowners of single-family dwellings, a number of factors—like the amount of shade, the pitch and integrity of the roof, steep upfront financial costs, long payback periods, and other building code limitations—may render solar panels difficult, if not cost prohibitive.  States like California also recently cut back the amount of net metering afforded to solar homeowners by 75% to 80%, with the passage of NEM 3.0.  So even if you do own your home, with a new roof, with optimal pitch and plenty of sunshine, the financial math may still not add up, depending on your consumption patterns and other circumstances. 


Some municipalities offer renewable energy purchasing programs, where consumers pay higher electricity prices in exchange for an assurance that their electricity came from renewable sources.  But those programs are not available in every city and county.  Nor do they constitute an investment.  While they help increase demand for renewable energy, the consumer does not own—and therefore does not reap the benefits from owning—the clean energy infrastructure behind it.



Locked out


Some private equity firms and infrastructure funds directly invest in large scale renewable energy projects, like large on-shore wind farms or utility-scale solar arrays.  However, those funds are limited to large institutional investors, like pension funds, university endowments, wealthy families, and other "accredited" investors.  Accredited investors are persons or entities allowed to invest in securities that are not registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission.  Under Rule 501 of Regulation D of the Securities Act of 1933, a natural person must have a net worth exceeding $1 million to qualify.  That figure excludes the value of a person’s primary residence and any indebtedness that exceeds the value of that home.  So, in short, unless you're a millionaire or a large institution, you're out of luck.  You are locked out of the opportunity to directly invest in clean energy infrastructure.

 

So what about the stock market? 

 

The stock market provides some opportunities to invest in clean energy, but the options are limited in two key ways.

 

First, about 50% of the United States' clean energy capacity is "off-market."  That is, it is not owned by a public company with shares that can be bought and sold on a U.S. exchange, like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE).  And second, among the publicly traded U.S. companies that do own renewable energy assets, 84% of them also invest in fossil fuel power plants, like coal- and gas-fired plants.  In other words, buying stock in your local investor-owned utility company almost virtually guarantees that you are also investing in fossil fuels, no matter how much they tout their “green” credentials.


We dive deeper in that analysis below.


Off-market and out-of-bounds 

 

Firn set out to understand just how much renewable energy in the U.S. was "off-market" and therefore out of reach for everyday, non-accredited investors. 

 

Using U.S. Energy Information Administration data, Firn compiled a database of all renewable energy assets owners, with assets rated over 1 MW.  Those assets included photovoltaic (PV) solar installations, concentrated solar power (CSP) installations, onshore wind farms, geothermal power plants, hydropower dams, and battery storage.  Many of those assets were owned outright by single private limited liability companies (LLCs), which were then owned by another parent company.  Often, those companies were themselves subsidiaries of larger multinational corporations.  By cross-referencing those ownership "trees'' with databases on corporate parent-subsidiary relationships, Firn mapped the true ownership of more than half the installed renewable energy capacity in the U.S., or around 253 GW of renewable energy.  Following the money, that exercise revealed who (or what) owned the nation's clean energy infrastructure and whether a normal investor could invest in it.  Here's what we found:

 

Around 75% of U.S. hydroelectric capacity is owned by a governmental entity or other non-traded entities, like cooperatives or irrigation districts.  More than 60% of geothermal and 50% of utility-scale battery storage capacity is also off-market (i.e., government owned or privately owned).  While public companies own 60% to 75% of the installed capacity for solar and wind in the U.S., a third of those companies are foreign domiciled.  Foreign domiciled companies present challenges for U.S. investors, since investing in foreign companies usually requires a bank to sponsor an American Depositary Receipt (ADR).  If an ADR does not exist, even steeper hurdles arise.  And, once invested, those foreign shares carry heightened liquidity risk—making the investments inappropriate for all but the most sophisticated investors.  And even if an ADR is available, U.S. investors must pay additional fees and foreign taxes—just to own a share of American renewable energy infrastructure!


Rarely a pure play


So what about those U.S. companies that are publicly traded and own renewable energy infrastructure?  When retail investors buy shares of a company on the stock market, the investor owns shares of the entire company, not just the parts they like.  For example, many investor-owned utilities own clean energy assets, like wind farms or utility scale solar installations.  That’s great!  But nearly all of them also own dirty fossil-fuel power plants.  That’s bad!  With few exceptions, investors choosing to buy shares of U.S. energy players have little choice but to invest in the whole portfolio, taking the bitter with the sweet—or in this case, the dirty with the clean.


Mutual funds are no real answer, either


In recent years, there has been a growth of “green” or “clean technology” mutual funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs).  While the emergence of those funds is a welcome sight, they, too, can only invest in the same pool of publicly traded companies (they will also pass on the costs of investing in any foreign companies, too).  A Firn analysis of a dozen leading “clean technology” ETFs and mutual funds revealed that they tend to invest more in upstream companies, like wind turbine or semiconductor manufacturers, than the companies that own the clean energy infrastructure outright.  Owning these funds is analogous to owning lumber mills, concrete manufacturers, and roofing companies in lieu of owning a house.  Of course, investing in companies that help build the clean energy transition—or, at least, that stand to gain from it—is an important part of a diversified portfolio. So if one wishes to invest in the industries adjacent to clean energy infrastructure, then these funds offer a path to doing so.  


But if one wishes to own the clean energy infrastructure outright, and earn revenue from that clean energy, then those mutual funds, regrettably, still provide no real answer. 



Why access matters


Everyday savers and retail investors are left with no way to make pure-play investments in the same clean energy assets that institutional investors, family offices, and other high-net worth individuals routinely use to diversify their portfolio.  Fixing this problem matters for two reasons.  


First, doing so could unleash hundreds of billions, if not trillions of dollars, towards renewable energy deployment.  In 2019, Americans—rich and poor—tucked away $9.34 trillion in traditional savings accounts.  By December 2022, even with pandemic stimulus relief fading into the rearview mirror, Americans had $19.8 trillion saved in savings, checking accounts, and retail money market accounts.  If even one-tenth of that cash could be invested into productive clean energy infrastructure through a platform like Firn, it would represent a 5x bigger investment than the combined total of all of the Inflation Reduction Act’s investments into clean energy (which, by contrast, was just under $400 billion).  Just imagine: no need to worry about who wins the next election cycle or whether Joe Manchin kills another climate bill.  Anyone can just open their app, click a button, and voila: they’ve directly funded more renewable energy.


Second, giving access to everyone addresses a fundamental equity concern.  Historically, the path to long-term wealth creation in capitalist societies begins with ownership, regardless if that occurs in the form of owning land, real estate, or stock in productive businesses.  


Too often, though, the story goes that the rich get richer, and the poor get poorer.  America is no exception.  And while America is a story of progress, rather than perfection, it remains a poignant fact that people of color and recent immigrants still disproportionately qualify as poor or lower-income.  It is a bitter irony, therefore, that the people who have the most to lose from climate change are also the ones least able to build wealth from the efforts trying to stop it.


Where Firn comes in


The solution to this access problem is simple in theory, but complicated in practice. We need to create a new asset class—one composed solely of clean energy infrastructure, one that is accessible by anyone.


That's where Firn comes in.  Leveraging recent changes in securities laws, Firn is building a platform to invest in solar, wind, battery, and other clean energy technology assets—directly.  No fossil fuels, and no foreign taxes.  We believe billions of dollars worth of capital can be unlocked to finance the clean energy transition and mitigate climate change.


The world needs to make investing in clean energy as simple as investing in real estate, if not easier.  The stakes are massive.  After all, only the fate of the world depends on it.
