A Critical Look at ESG

  • Date : January 23, 2023
  • Time : 3 Min Read


If you have been following investment trends in the last few years, you will almost certainly have come across the acronym “ESG” or the phrase “ESG investing.”  ESG stands for environmental, social, and corporate governance.  Originally, the idea behind ESG was something similar to impact investing.  By investing in companies with “good” environmental, social, and governance records, and avoiding companies with “bad” records, you could do more to improve society–or, at least, sleep better at night.  Early ESG funds invested in an array of companies, but tended to exclude social pariahs like tobacco companies, casinos, or firms linked to apartheid South Africa.  


While ESG investing has long been a darling of progressive or socially conscious investors, it has recently become a darling of Wall Street as well.  As ESG surged in popularity, more and more asset managers began to advertise their funds as “ESG.”  Dressed in the language of sustainability and social activism, proponents of ESG across the financial world argued that investors could have the best of both worlds: ESG investments would save the world while outperforming the market.  Customers bit, and ESG investments rocketed up, growing at a rate of over 26% per annum.


As a result of those trends, ESG investing has become a bit of a sacred cow in corporate circles.  Major companies continue to hire sustainability consultants and in-house corporate social responsibility (“CSR”) teams, even as layoffs loom in some sectors. Critics of ESG investing have even been fired from their posts for publicly questioning its merits.


A reader may reasonably expect Firn–a sustainable investment platform–to be unequivocally supportive of ESG investing.  But we are not.  While we find that the motivations behind ESG investing are generally heartening (i.e., a commitment to values, to social impact, and to understanding risks that go beyond the balance sheet), we believe that the way in which ESG investing is currently marketed, understood, and implemented leaves much to be desired.

Deconstructing a sacred cow


There are many flaws with the concept of ESG.  Let’s start with the acronym.  


First, the categories of “ESG” are–to be blunt–remarkably incoherent.  When building a framework, best practice suggests that sub-categories remain “mutually exclusive and comprehensively exhaustive.”  In other words, a framework’s sub-categories should cover all cases for which the framework is meant to apply, without the sub-categories overlapping or bleeding into one another.


ESG violates that cardinal rule of structured thinking.  For example, how would one categorize a waste management company’s decision to site a landfill or incinerator in a poor or marginalized community?  Is that an environmental issue? A social issue? Kind of both? 70-30?  60-40? 50-50?  Such an example illustrates how a company’s environmental record can absolutely be a social issue, and how a company’s approach to social responsibility can absolutely impact the environment.  Separating out the blurry category of environmental issues from the even blurrier category of social issues, in practice, becomes fraught with arbitrary line-drawing and risks ignoring or compartmentalizing the crucial links between environmental harms and social inequities.


Similarly, the “G” of “ESG”–governance-based investing–too often collapses into just another form of social issue-based investing.  The prototypical governance issue for most ESG funds is whether or not a company’s board of directors appears sufficiently diverse in terms of race and gender.  Arguably, that issue is just as much a social issue as it is a governance issue (and vice versa).  


We can take this critique of governance-based investing a bit further.  Since most ESG investing occurs in the public markets, investing with a governance lens rarely offers any source of differentiation among companies, as the vast majority of publicly traded companies are incorporated as Delaware C-corporations (including two-thirds of Fortune 500 companies).  Unless one can invest in private markets, where one can find a smattering of LLCs, LPs, benefit corporations, S-corporations, and other legal entities (like MLPs, general partnerships, and so on), an entire category of “ESG” does very little to help an investor discriminate between one company and another.  What good is such a lens if it filters out nothing?  Even when one broadens the view beyond governance structures on paper to look at governance practices, the result is largely the same.  Notwithstanding some high profile cases where a regulator faulted a company’s board of directors for its poor oversight (like Wells Fargo with respect to its 2016 sales practices scandal), few corporate boards make the news. Without insider information, it’s difficult to know which companies’ boards are doing well and which are asleep at the wheel.  Therefore, when ESG investing is implemented in practice, the entire category of governance-based investing usually becomes little more than a microcosm of its sister category of social-based investing.



Whose ESG?

Even if we ditched the acronym (or, better yet, we improved it somehow), the crux of ESG investing still constitutes a form of values-based investing.   As such, it raises a fundamental question: whose values get prioritized?


With the surge of enthusiasm that came with ESG investing, so came the backlash: anti-ESG.  Several fund managers have staked their reputation combatting what they decry as political favoritism and “woke-ism” infecting their investment ranks.


Anti-ESG, in our opinion, is a misnomer.  Anti-ESG is really just ESG investing with reverse polarity.  After all, anti-ESG funds still consider environmental, social, and governance factors when investing; they simply weight those factors in the opposite direction as conventional ESG funds, which superficially appear more aligned with progressive values (at least, on paper).


However one feels about the rise of anti-ESG, it serves a useful purpose in highlighting the vacuous nature of ESG as a framework.  At bottom, ESG can sort of mean whatever the fund manager wants it to mean.  Indeed, many ESG funds do not disclose the ESG factors that they consider beyond those generic labels. Other funds offer a laundry list of factors, but those descriptions, too, tend to be capacious and vague.  Firn is not aware of any ESG funds that disclose how various factors are weighted relative to each other, let alone describe how those factors are measured with externally verifiable data.



“Do as I say, not as I do”


Keeping things vague, of course, benefits Wall Street firms that enjoy the upside from selling ESG-labeled funds (namely, more fees), but dislike the consequences (namely, being held accountable for impact).  As the SEC contemplates more rule-making around ESG disclosures, those consequences may soon include bringing some degree of accountability on fund managers and their investment decisions. Fund managers that hold out certain criteria as part of their investment analysis, but then fail to follow them, may soon become vulnerable to prosecution or other class action litigation.  Accordingly, the fuzzier the ESG criteria are, the easier it is to avoid being pinned down. 


An even more immediate consequence that fund managers would like to avoid is suboptimal performance.  If they can shoehorn all sorts of politically unsavory but high-performing companies into their funds and still call it ESG, they can worry less about underperforming the market; and therefore, they can worry less about losing customers and losing fees.   


That theory has surprising empirical support.  In November 2022, the Swiss Finance Institute published a research paper looking at how often ESG mutual fund managers invested their own wealth into the funds they managed.  After reviewing 1,216 actively managed U.S. equity funds, the researchers found that nearly a quarter of those mutual funds’ managers never invested a single penny into their ESG funds.  Of those that did, fund managers invested less into the ones that performed higher on conventional ESG metrics.  In other words, they tended to invest more when their funds’ portfolio companies were dirtier or meaner or just overall less ESG-friendly.  The analysis revealed that “fund managers do not believe ESG factors to be a positive driver of financial performance.”  The authors concluded:

Overall, our analyses indicate that fund managers do not expect ESG strategies to deliver higher risk-adjusted performance. This suggests that the popularity of these strategies in the mutual fund industry is primarily driven by client demand, that is, by the possibility of fund managers to attract higher flows by tilting their portfolios in a higher-ESG direction. 

Translated, fund managers dress their funds in ESG-speak to entice more investment, but they don’t believe their own advertising enough to put their own skin in the game.  Why trust a chef who will not eat his own cooking?


The map to impact


At Firn, we believe financial markets can effectively drive positive environmental impact.  Doing so starts with creating a coherent conceptual map of how investing decisions can move the needle on environmental challenges, like climate change, air pollution, water pollution, and biodiversity loss.  It can equally be applied to social issues, including cross-cutting issues like environmental justice.  


Our conceptual approach evaluates different styles of impact investing across two key dimensions: feasibility and impact.

The low impact approaches

In the upper left hand quadrant, “screen and divest” takes the approach of refusing to invest in, or divesting from, bad companies.  It is here that one would place the vast majority of ESG funds, which tilt towards pollution-lite sectors like software and technology.  We surmise that this approach is the most predominant because it requires so little effort.  A fund manager can take a basket of stocks–say, the S&P 500–and apply relatively straightforward criteria to exclude companies.


Yet, the “screen and divest” approach suffers from two major drawbacks.  First, would-be shareholders of “bad” companies no longer have any ability to influence those companies through shareholder voting or shareholder activism.  Second, screening out companies from a broad index approach increases the risk that the portfolio’s returns will be suboptimal.  Even in actively managed funds, having fewer companies available in a manager’s investable universe necessarily constrains her degrees of freedom, which may limit her to oversubscribed stocks that trade at a premium to their intrinsic value. That’s fine and well when tides are rising, but it can create whiplash in tumultuous markets. Case in point: while tech stocks were flying high in the late bull market run of 2018 through 2021, ESG funds outperformed.  But when growth companies took a large hit with tightening interest rates, those same ESG funds grossly underperformed.


In the lower left hand quadrant, there is another approach that some ESG investors use: what we dub “best in class.”  With this approach, investors look at companies across different sectors and invest in the companies that are doing the best relative to their peers.  That way, the theory goes, an ESG investor can encourage companies to improve their environmental footprint or corporate social responsibility. 


We find this approach to be even less impactful, as it shares many of the flaws of screening and divesting (i.e., you relinquish shareholder control of the “worst of the worst” – the companies, which, ironically, likely need the most shareholder activism), and it can even work across purposes with the screen and divest approach.  


Here’s how that works. By definition, there will be some oil and gas company that performs slightly better on ESG metrics than other oil and gas companies.  Under the “best in class approach,” some investors would be justified in investing in oil and gas companies, even when others find it more appropriate to divest from oil and gas companies altogether!  When different ESG investment approaches work across purposes like that, it undermines the encouragement (or opprobrium) that investment (or lack of investment) is meant to convey.  The signal simply gets lost in the noise.  And that assumes such a signal could ever get translated through a stock price in the first place–a doubtful assumption, in our opinion.


Another drawback of the “best in class” approach is the sheer volume of accurate data needed to rank, sort, and assess companies across various metrics.  How does one know that Shell’s carbon footprint or water usage is lower than British Petroleum?  Self-reported data?  Sure, that’s reliable.  What’s about Scope 3 emissions for a company like Apple?  When more than half of its supply chain comes from overseas, where the companies remain under no obligation to account for their carbon emissions, how do we know that Apple is a “greener” company than one of its competitors?  Perhaps all of those data are out there; and perhaps there are “good enough” heuristics to help fill in the missing gaps.  But it’s sure a lot of effort for an impact investor to undertake for, well, not a lot of impact.


The better approaches

In our opinion, there are two other approaches that offer more impact.  The more straightforward approach is shareholder activism.


Every shareholder gets to vote in the annual shareholder meeting, whether in person or by proxy vote (or, at least, that’s true for the vast majority of publicly traded corporations).  If one owns enough of a company, she may even introduce resolutions for other shareholders to vote on.
